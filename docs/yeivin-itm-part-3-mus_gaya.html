Musical Gaʿya

318

The names “heavy gaʿya” and “light gaʿya” (געיה כבדה and געיה קלה) for the two basic categories of musical gaʿya were introduced by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan, and were, as Dotan has remarked, an unfortunate innovation. Dotan, in his edition of the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim uses the terms “small gaʿya” and “great gaʿya” (געיה קטנה and געיה גדולה), terms which are used in the Masorah. These terms are not appropriate either, for although in the Masorah the term “small gaʿya” is used for gaʿya on a closed syllable, it may also include gaʿya on an open syllable, and it seems quite likely that the term “great gaʿya” in the Masorah refers to an accent. For this reason the descriptive names “gaʿya on a closed syllable” and “gaʿya on an open syllable” are used here.

Gaʿya on a Closed Syllable

319

Regular Structure. A word of the same structure as מִתְקַטְּלִים, which has a closed syllable, separated from the stress syllable by another (the “buffer” syllable) followed by vocal shewa (simple or ḥaṭef ) is said to have “regular” structure. In words of such structure, the initial closed syllable has gaʿya if the word has a disjunctive accent, as נִֽתְחַכְּמָ֖ה (Ex 1:10). In the pattern מִתְקַטְּלִים, the vocal shewa stands under a letter with dagesh “forte” (follows a doubled consonant). It may also be the second of two shewa s, in a form of pattern מִתְפַּלְפְּלִים, as הַֽכַּרְמְלִֽי (1S 30:5), or a ḥaṭef shewa in a form of pattern מִתְפַּעֲלִים as מִֽשְׁתַּחֲוִ֖ים (Gen 37:9). “Fully Regular” Structure

Words of the patterns described in #319 are said to have “fully regular” structure. “Not Fully Regular” Structure

322

Words with patterns similar to those described in #319 but showing slight differences in structure, are said to be of “not fully regular” structure. Words in this category show slightly less tendency to use gaʿya than words with fully regular structure. The following patterns are included in this category:-

1) Where the closed syllable is followed by a vocal shewa as

1C 2:3הַֽכְּנַעֲנִ֑ית
Ex 16:23אֲשֶֽׁר־תְּבַשְּׁלוּ֙

2) Where the “buffer” syllable has a long vowel, such as qameṣ, followed by a ḥaṭef shewa as

qameṣJos 10:36וַיִּֽלָּחֲמ֖וּ

ḥolem1K 8:42וּֽזְרֹעֲךָ֖
ḥolemRut 2:20מִֽגֹּאֲלֵ֖נוּ
ḥolemLev 23:44אֶֽת־מֹעֲדֵ֖י

Words with patterns like הַפּוֹעֲלִים (as הַכֹּהֲנִים, הַשֹּׁעֲרִים, etc.) usually do not have gaʿya and in general the number of exceptions to the rule in this category is much greater than among words with fully regular structure.

3) Where the “buffer” syllable has a long vowel, followed by simple shewa (as in מִתְבָּרְכִים) gaʿya is generally not marked. There are, however, a few exceptions, as

Dt 30:14בִּֽלְבָבְךָ֖(and elsewhere)
Lev 19:5לִֽרְצֹנְכֶ֖ם(and elsewhere)
2S 5:1וּֽבְשָׂרְךָ֖

4) Where the stress syllable is preceded by a ḥaṭef—or even a simple shewa—which separates two identical consonants (or a ḥaṭef under some other non-guttural letter). If this ḥaṭef is preceded by a short vowel, gaʿya is marked as in words with fully regular structure, as

Jud 16:24וַֽיְהַלְל֖וּ
1K 8:30יִֽתְפַּלְל֖וּ

If the vowel preceding the ḥaṭef is long, the marking of gaʿya follows the rule given in (2) above, as

Zeph 2:1הִֽתְקוֹשְׁשׁ֖וּ
Jos 22:6וַֽיְבָרְכֵ֖ם
Dt 32:6וַֽיְכֹנְנֶֽךָ

323

Gaʿya is sometimes marked on a closed syllable in words of similar structure, but where the stress syllable is not preceded by shewa or ḥaṭef, but the buffer syllable has, or could have, gaʿya.

  1. Words composed of a closed syllable followed by some form from the root היה or חיה which could have gaʿya on the first syllable (see #355), as Is 10:22 אִם־יִהְיֶ֞ה—bN has אִֽם־.
  2. Words in which the buffer syllable is marked with gaʿya before a guttural with shewa (#354), as 1C 5:32 וּֽזְרַֽחְיָ֖ה the first gaʿya is musical, the second phonetic). Also
1C 27:19יִֽשְׁמַעְיָ֖הוּ
1S 26:19יִֽשְׁמַֽע־נָא֙
Jer 5:7אֶֽסְלַֽח־לָ֔ךְכתיב אסלוח

It would be possible to include words of pattern מִתְבָּרְכִים (class 3 in #322) in this group, since the buffer syllable could have gaʿya (on an open syllable) in those too.

Gaʿya in words of regular structure is the most common use of gaʿya on a closed syllable.

Non-regular structure

324

Words with structure which is not “regular”—that is not patterned like מִתְקַטְּלִים or its variants described above—rarely show gaʿya on a closed syllable, and the use of gaʿya on such words has no fixed rules.

1) In some 15 cases gaʿya is marked on the fourth syllable before the accent, as Dt 12:2 אֶֽת־כׇּל־הַמְּקֹמ֞וֹת.

2) In some 200 cases gaʿya is marked on the third syllable before the accent. This occurs mainly when the accent is one of the “high” trilled disjunctives (#195). Thus with

Ex 15:20כׇֽל־הַנָּשִׁים֙

And even with conjunctives, as

In some 30 cases gaʿya is marked on the second syllable before the accent--most commonly on a word with penultimate stress marked with pashṭa, as

Gaʿya before Paseq

325

This is most commonly found in cases like 1K 2:30 וַיֹּ֥אמֶֽר ׀ לֹ֖א, where it is evidently used to mark a separation between the two words greater than that indicated by the paseq. Gaʿya is only marked in this position in early MSS, and is rare even there.

Gaʿya on an Open Syllable, or a syllable with a Long Vowel before Shewa

326

Gaʿya may be marked on an open syllable with either a long or a short vowel, and also on a syllable marked with a long vowel before shewa. This is the gaʿya called “light” by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan, and “great” by Dotan. The Masoretes regarded shewa after a long vowel within a word as silent (לא יצא בפה see #386) so that in a word like קָֽטְלוּ the syllable marked by gaʿya is, in their view, closed, not open. However because the rules for gaʿya in this situation are similar to those for its use on an open syllable, the two situations are described together. Note, however, that two different types of syllable are involved.

This section, then, describes the use of gaʿya (i) on an open syllable before a full vowel, as אָֽנֹכִ֖י (ii) on an open syllable before a ḥaṭef shewa as שָֽׁאֲלָ֖ה and יַֽעֲמֹ֖ד (iii) on a syllable with a long vowel before a shewa as קָֽטְלוּ. The use of gaʿya in these cases is not affected by the accent on the word. It occurs both with disjunctive and conjunctive accents. Thus:-

330

The use of gaʿya on an open syllable is not described in the masoretic literature or in other early sources, and there are only scattered references to this gaʿya. In the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim (Baer-Strack 1879, #32) it is said

י(י)ראוmeaning “fear” has gaʿya asGen 20:8וַיִּֽירְא֥וּ
יראוmeaning “see” has no gaʿya asNu 17:24וַיִּרְא֥וּ

In the Horayat ha-Qore p. 90 (Dérenbourg, 1870, p. 398) it is said that

Gaʿya sometimes distinguishes meaning, as in the rule “forms from ירא have gaʿya, forms from ראה do not” and as in תִּשְׁנ֕וּ (Neh 13:21) which means “do again” (שנה) and יִֽ֭שְׁנוּ (Prov 4:16) which means “sleep” (ישן). Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan was the first to draw up rules for the use of this gaʿya, and he has been followed by later scholars.

331

Gaʿya on an open syllable, or after a long vowel followed by a shewa is the most common category of gaʿya in the Bible, and occurs in thousands of words. In the early MSS, this gaʿya is not marked on all the words where it could be, but only on some of them, and in some MSS it is marked more commonly than in others. It seems probably that it was not considered important to mark it—possibly because it made less difference to the pronunciation of the word than did gaʿya on a closed syllable.

In A, this gaʿya is most commonly marked on words with pashṭa or zaqef, less commonly on those with other disjunctives, and only rarely on words with conjunctives. It is marked in about 30% of the possible cases.

In L and S it is marked in about 40% of the possible cases. In B and S1 it is marked in about 20% of the possible cases. In C this gaʿya is marked much more commonly—in about 75% of the possible cases.

The other early MSS mark this gaʿya in varying proportions of the possible cases. It is not always true that earlier MSS mark it less, as is shown by B and C, which are roughly contemporary. It is, however, generally true that later MSS mark this gaʿya more commonly, and the printed texts mark gaʿya on an open syllable regularly on every syllable suitable for it.

Gaʿya on an Open Syllable after the Accent

332

A special case of gaʿya on an open syllable is gaʿya marked after the accent. If a word with the accent on the penultimate syllable ends in an open syllable, and stands before a word with the accent on the first syllable, gaʿya may be marked on the open syllable after the accent at the end of the first word, as

2K 1:13עֲבָדֶ֥יךָֽ אֵ֛לֶּה
1S 15:6עָשִׂ֤יתָֽה חֶ֙סֶד֙
Jer 9:20עָ֤לָֽה מָ֙וֶת֙
Is 52:11ס֤וּרֽוּ ס֙וּרוּ֙
Ez 21:16הוּחַ֤דָּֽה חֶ֙רֶב֙
1C 12:26גִּבּ֤וֹרֵֽי חַ֙יִל֙
Is 14:31הֵילִ֤ילִֽי שַׁ֙עַר֙

This gaʿya is only rarely marked. It is most common in early MSS, but occurs only in scattered places in them.

This gaʿya occurs more often where the vowel of the stress syllable of the second word is preceded by shewa, as

Gen 28:2פַּדֶּ֣נָֽה אֲרָ֔ם(and elsewhere)
Dt 32:13וַיֵּנִקֵ֤הֽוּ דְבַשׁ֙
Ez 41:7לְמַ֨עְלָֽה לְמַ֜עְלָה(2× in this verse!)
Jer 9:18בֹּ֤שְׁנֽוּ מְאֹד֙

This gaʿya also is marked most in early MSS, and is not marked in printed texts.

Gaʿya with Shewa

333

Gaʿya marked with shewa (or a ḥaṭef ) at the beginning of a word indicates (as elsewhere) that the syllable must be slowed or lengthened. Consequently that shewa becomes a vowel—probably equivalent to an ordinary short vowel. This gaʿya is rare in the twenty one books (only some 200 cases occur) but it is common in the three books. This is a musical gaʿya, but it is often used with shewa before a guttural, which suggests that there may be phonetic reasons for its use. The system of marking it is similar to that for gaʿya on a closed syllable, but there are no firm rules for its use.

This gaʿya is marked on the second, third, or fourth syllable before the accent. It is usually used with “high” accents (#195), and so with geresh.

It also occasionally occurs with other accents as

(where gaʿya with shewa is given preference over gaʿya on a closed syllable in a word with “regular” structure).

Gaʿya is also used on shewa before the vowel of the syllable immediately before the stress syllable. This occurs mostly on words with zaqef, as

The above describes gaʿya with shewa in words with disjunctive accents. This gaʿya also occurs occasionally in words with conjunctives, especially unusual combinations of conjunctives, as Ez 34:10

Occasionally gaʿya with shewa is used in words with other conjunctives, as with

In the three poetical books there are a few cases of gaʿya with shewa within a word, as

336

Gaʿya with shewa is regularly marked in early MSS but the rules for it are not described in early sources, nor even by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan. Heidenheim and Baer were the first to establish these rules. The pronunciation of shewa with gaʿya is described in the masoretic literature (as in the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim (Baer-Strack 1879, #11)—see also Morag, 1963, p. 160 ff.). They state that shewa at the beginning of a word was generally pronounced as a small pataḥ (פתחה קטנה) but that when it was marked with gaʿya it was pronounced like a full pataḥ (בפתחה גדולה תצא). Before gutturals, the shewa was pronounced as a short vowel of the same quality as the vowel following the guttural, but when it was marked with gaʿya it had the sound of a full vowel of that quality (thus בְּֽהֹנוֹת֩ Jud 1:7 would be pronounced with three ḥolem s). Shewa at the beginning of a word before yod was pronounced like a short ḥireq, but when marked with a gaʿya, it was pronounced like a full ḥireq.

Gaʿya in a Closed Syllable with a Long Vowel

337

Gaʿya may be marked in a closed syllable with a long vowel. In Hebrew such syllables usually have the accent, and when they do not have the accent they usually have maqqef. In the last situation, gaʿya may be marked before either a disjunctive or a conjunctive accent.

This gaʿya is marked regularly in most printed texts, but in MSS it is usually marked only if the closed syllable with the long vowel stands immediately before the stress syllable, as

Hos 8:7אֵֽין־ל֗וֹ
Jud 17:7גָֽר־שָֽׁם
Jos 12:16בֵּֽית־אֵ֖ל
2K 4:26רֽוּץ־נָ֣א

If the closed syllable in question is the second before the accent, gaʿya is usually not marked, as

Gen 41:45שֵׁם־יוֹסֵף֮
Jos 23:6סוּר־מִמֶּ֖נּוּ
Jos 15:41בֵּית־דָּג֥וֹן
Jos 3:3בְּרִית־יְהֹוָה֙

If the syllable in question is the third before the accent, gaʿya is more likely to be marked, as

Jos 9:7אִֽישׁ־יִשְׂרָאֵ֖ל
Rut 2:23קְצִֽיר־הַשְּׂעֹרִ֖ים
Jud 2:5שֵֽׁם־הַמָּק֥וֹם(and elsewhere)

In general the marking of this gaʿya in the early MSS is more regular than the marking of gaʿya on an open syllable, but less regular than the marking of gaʿya on a closed syllable of the “regular” type. However the regularity varies from MS to MS.

Gaʿya on a closed syllable with Ṣere after the Accent

338

Where a word ending in a closed syllable pointed with ṣere has its accent retracted, and the ṣere remains, it is marked by gaʿya (see #308, 1c), as

Nu 17:23וַיָּ֣צֵֽץ צִ֔יץ
Is 66:3עֹ֣רֵֽף כֶּ֔לֶב
Is 66:3מְבָ֣רֵֽךְ אָ֑וֶן
Is 40:7נָ֣בֵֽל צִ֔יץcf. 40:8

This gaʿya is marked both in MSS and in printed texts.

The System of Preference for Musical Gaʿya

339

In early MSS, such as A, L, C, two gaʿya s are only rarely marked on the same word (or on a group of words joined by maqqef ). Where a word or word group could have more than one gaʿya, only one of them is generally marked as

Ezra 9:12בְּֽנוֹתֵיכֶ֞ם
Jer 31:20אֲֽרַחֲמֶ֖נּוּ(31:18 or 19 in some editions)

where gaʿya is marked under the initial shewa but not in the following open syllable, or Ex 15:26 כׇּֽל־הַמַּחֲלָ֞ה, where gaʿya in the initial closed syllable (a “non-regular” type) is marked, while that of the regular type, under the he, is not. Contrariwise, in A in 2K 23:12 וְאֶת־הַֽמִּזְבְּח֡וֹת, gaʿya on a closed syllable of the regular type is marked, while that of the non-regular type, under the alef and gaʿya with the initial shewa, are not. (The gaʿya with the shewa was marked by bN.)

340

The choice of which of two or more gaʿya s should be marked under a word is determined by certain general principles, although these are affected by many detailed considerations which cannot be described here. The basic principle is that gaʿya on a closed syllable is given preference over gaʿya on an open syllable, or gaʿya after a long vowel followed by shewa. Examples are:-

Jud 18:12וַֽיַּעֲל֗וּ
Jud 19:29הַֽמַּאֲכֶ֙לֶת֙

Here gaʿya on the initial closed syllable (of the regular type) is marked, but that on the following open syllable (before the ḥaṭef ) is not. So also

Dt 31:20אֶֽל־הָאֲדָמָ֣ה׀
Nu 17:21כׇּֽל־נְשִׂיאֵיהֶ֡ם

Here gaʿya is marked on the initial closed syllable of non-regular type, but not on the following open syllable. This general principle is common to all the early MSS.

On the problem of preference in words which could have more than one gaʿya on an open syllable (or on a syllable with a long vowel followed by shewa) see #328.

341

In the case of words which could have gaʿya on an open syllable and on a closed syllable with a long vowel (#337) the general rule is that if the closed syllable comes immediately before the stress syllable, gaʿya is marked on it, as

Rut 1:11הַעֽוֹד־לִ֤י
Job 2:11לָנֽוּד־ל֖וֹ

However if this is not the case, the gaʿya on the open syllable is preferred, as

Is 8:11הָֽעָם־הַזֶּ֖ה(and elsewhere)
Jos 11:12הַמְּלָֽכִים־הָ֠אֵ֠לֶּה
Dt 7:1גּֽוֹיִם־רַבִּ֣ים׀ אא֡א

The Marking of Gaʿya in Manuscripts and Printed Texts

342

In early MSS, gaʿya on a closed syllable of regular or non-regular type, and gaʿya with shewa, are usually carefully and accurately marked. In some MSS this is also the case with gaʿya on a closed syllable with a long vowel. MSS following this system show the following characteristics:

  1. The system of marking these gaʿya s is generally consistent throughout the MS.
  2. The system of marking these gaʿya s is similar in different MSS. That is, gaʿya is generally marked in the same position on the same word in nearly all MSS.
  3. Variations involving gaʿya noted in lists of ḥillufim or in marginal notes in the MSS are almost solely concerned with gaʿya in these categories.

These characteristics show the importance to the Masoretes of gaʿya in these categories, as opposed to gaʿya in an open syllable (or after a long vowel followed by shewa). The lesser importance of gaʿya in these latter categories is also shown by the fact that

  1. The system of marking gaʿya on an open syllable (or after a long vowel followed by shewa) is not consistent in any single MS.
  2. The early MSS do not share the same system for marking gaʿya in these categories, and they show no uniformity in the words on which gaʿya in these categories is marked.
  3. Lists of ḥillufim are almost bare of examples of variants involving gaʿya in these categories.

343

In later MSS and in printed texts, a different system of marking gaʿya is used. In printed texts, gaʿya on an open syllable (or after a long vowel followed by shewa) is generally consistently and systematically marked, but gaʿya on a closed syllable is marked only sporadically, or not at all.

344

This difference is reflected in the system of preference for marking gaʿya. If a word could have gaʿya both on a closed syllable and on an open syllable, that on the open syllable is marked in printed text, as Ex 14:19 וַיַּֽעֲמֹ֖ד whereas that on the closed syllable would be marked in early MSS, i.e. וַֽיַּעֲמֹ֖ד.

In a number of scholarly editions, such as those of Baer, Heidenheim, Qoren (Koren), and others, gaʿya as completely as possible, so that both gaʿya s are marked in a word like וַֽיַּֽעֲמֹ֖ד. Similar usage is found in a number of MSS, as noted by Eliahu ha-Levi in his Ṭuv Ṭaʿam chapter 7:

When there is dagesh in the second letter of a word, and this letter is followed by א, ה, ח, or ע pointed with ḥaṭef pataḥ, then that word has two metheg s: one on the first letter — the principal metheg, one on the letter before the guttural—but only if the word has a disjunctive accent, as וַֽיַּֽעֲמֹד and וַֽיַּֽעֲבֹד.
