Phonetic Gaʿya

345

Some gaʿya s are not affected by the syllabic structure of the word, or by its accent. They have a phonetic, not a musical function. They have several purposes, among them gaʿya with a short vowel (or even a long vowel) to show that a following shewa is vocal, and gaʿya before a guttural to slow down the reading of the vowel so that the guttural may be pronounced properly. It is not always clear what particular fault in pronunciation phonetic gaʿya was intended to remedy. However the effect of phonetic gaʿya is the same as that of musical gaʿya: the slowing down of the reading.

Gaʿya Marking a Following Shewa as Vocal

346

This gaʿya is marked in various situations:

  • before a consonant which has lost its historical doubling,
  • before a sibilant consonant,
  • before a group of similar consonants,
  • etc.

In some cases not only is the vowel before such a consonant marked with gaʿya, but a ḥaṭef is marked after it as well (#391), but this is not done consistently either with words of the same structure, or with the same words in different MSS. Some MSS mark the ḥaṭef frequently (and this is one of the unique characteristics of MsA). Others mark it rarely. The quality of this ḥaṭef is determined by the Tiberian rules for the pronunciation of vocal shewa (#336, #387).

  • Before a non-guttural other than yod, or a guttural pointed with pataḥ, ḥaṭef pataḥ is used.
  • Before a guttural pointed with qameṣ, ḥaṭef qameṣ is used.
  • Such a ḥaṭef is unlikely to occur before a guttural pointed with other vowels, or before yod.

Gaʿya on a Short Vowel

347

(i) On initial ה with pataḥ before מ. Gaʿya is often marked before mem under word-initial he with pataḥ representing either the definite article or he “interrogative”. Gaʿya is generally not marked if the he begins the syllable immediately before the stress syllable, as הַמְעַ֤ט (Is 7:13), and similarly it is not marked if the he begins the third syllable before the stress syllable, as הַמְקֻשָּׁרוֹת֒ (Gen 30:41). However if the syllable in question is the second before the stress syllable, gaʿya generally is marked, as

2S 10:3הַֽמְכַבֵּ֨ד
Is 40:20הַֽמְסֻכָּ֣ן
1C 27:21הַֽמְנַשֶּׁה֙
Job 3:21הַֽמְחַכִּ֣ים
Nu 20:19בַּֽמְסִלָּ֣ה

In all cases but the last (which is not preserved), MsA has ḥaṭef pataḥ under the mem.

In some cases, where simple shewa is used under the mem, where the word in question fits the description of regular structure, suitable for gaʿya on an initial closed syllable, as

Ez 46:24הַֽמְבַשְּׁלִ֔יםMsA
2C 32:31הַֽמְשַׁלְּחִ֤יםMsL( MsA מֲ )

It is uncertain whether the gaʿya is a phonetic gaʿya, in which case the following shewa is vocal, or a musical gaʿya, in which case the following shewa is silent. However, where ḥaṭef pataḥ is used under the mem, as in MsA in 2C 32:31, it is certain that the gaʿya is phonetic.

There are a number of exceptions to this rule, in which gaʿya is not marked before mem, as לַמְנַצֵּ֖חַ (Hab 3:19, and so always). In some other words gaʿya is not marked and the mem has dagesh (and so the shewa is vocal) as Jos 19:13 הַמְּתֹאָ֖ר and Qoh 11:5 הַמְּלֵאָ֑ה.

Gaʿya before the First of a Pair of Identical Consonants

351

Gaʿya is usually marked in this situation whether the first of the pair of consonants was historically doubled (and has lost this doubling) or not. Thus, historically doubled:

Is 62:9וְהִֽלְל֖וּ
Is 64:10הִֽלְל֙וּךָ֙
Jud 5:11מְחַֽצְצִ֗ים
2S 16:7בְּקַֽלְל֑וֹ
1S 2:25וּפִֽלְל֣וֹ
Ez 31:6קִֽנְנוּ֙

With no historical doubling:

Ez 4:12בְּגֶֽלְלֵי֙
2C 20:2בְּחַֽצְצ֣וֹן
Jud 9:57קִֽלְלַ֖ת
Mic 6:7בְּרִֽבְב֖וֹת
Zech 11:3יִֽלְלַ֣ת

In most such cases MsA marks a ḥaṭef shewa under the first of the two consonants.

In a few cases of this phenomenon gaʿya is not marked, as הִנְנִי (always), and Job 10:15 אַלְלַ֬י, and in some similar cases, the first of the two consonants has dagesh, and no gaʿya is marked, as

1K 1:40מְחַלְּלִ֣ים
Is 34:15קִנְּנָ֤ה
Ps 95:1נְרַנְּנָ֣ה

Gaʿya on a Long Vowel

352

A syllable containing a long vowel followed by shewa generally has musical gaʿya (#326). However in some MSS ḥaṭef shewa is used in a number of such cases, so that it can be assumed that in a number of cases gaʿya under a long vowel followed by shewa is phonetic gaʿya. Examples before ר are:

Job 31:37אֲקָֽרְבֶֽנּוּ
Nu 16:32כׇּל־הָרְכֽוּשׁ( MsB, MsL הָרֲ, MsS הֽ͏ָרְ )

and so often in forms from the roots ברך, גרשׁ, ירד (#379). Before other consonants:

Jer 22:28הֽוּטְלוּ֙( MsA טֲ )
Ez 9:8וְנֵֽאשְׁאַ֖ר
Est 1:6רִֽצְפַ֥ת( many MSS צֲ )

and so also in certain circumstances in forms from the roots אכל, הלך, and others (see #380).

353

Even where a long vowel is followed by two identical consonants the first of which is pointed with shewa, the long vowel is often marked with gaʿya, and the shewa is a ḥaṭef, as

Ez 26:12וּבָֽזְזוּ֙( MsA זֲ )
Ez 39:10
Is 58:9מִתּֽוֹכְךָ֙( MsA כֲ )
Jer 6:6סֹֽלְלָ֑ה( MsA לֲ )

This usage is not consistent, however.

  • Sometimes both gaʿya and ḥaṭef appear.
  • Sometimes gaʿya is not marked, and the shewa is simple.
  • Sometimes there is no gaʿya, but a ḥaṭef shewa is used,
  • and sometimes gaʿya is used, but the shewa is simple.

Different methods are used in different MSS, and there is no agreement between them. It appears that:

  • the general rule was that shewa after a long vowel was silent,
  • but that if this shewa stood under the first of two identical consonants it was always vocal. This latter fact could be marked either by a ḥaṭef shewa or by gaʿya, so
    • either one may be used alone,
    • but sometimes both are used, and
    • sometimes no indication is given.

(See #385).

Gaʿya on a Short Vowel before a Guttural

354

(i) Gaʿya before a Guttural which closes a Syllable. Gaʿya is sometimes marked before a guttural pointed with shewa either within a word or at the end of a word joined to a second by maqqef—especially if the second word begins with lamed or nun. Thus,

1C 5:4שְׁמַֽעְיָ֥הwithin word
Is 1:1יְשַֽׁעְיָ֣הוּ...
Neh 11:24וּפְתַֽחְיָ֨ה...
1K 1:51יִשָּׁבַֽע־לִ֤יbefore maqqef
Jud 14:3קַֽח־לִ֔י...
1S 28:22שְׁמַֽע־נָ֤א...

Gaʿya is similarly sometimes used on the last syllable of a word with penultimate stress if it ends with a guttural and the following word begins with lamed or nun, as

Rut 1:21הֵ֥רַֽע לִֽי
1K 2:8וָאֶשָּׁ֨בַֽע ל֤וֹ
Ez 1:4וְנֹ֥גַֽהּ ל֖וֹ
Ez 1:27

Gaʿya may even be marked in a similar situation after furtive pataḥ, as

Jud 19:25לִשְׁמֹ֣עַֽ ל֔וֹ
Dt 29:19סְלֹ֣חַֽ לוֹ֒

This gaʿya is consistently used with the following numbers (revia used as a generic accent):

  • אַרְבַּֽע־עֶשְׂרֵ֗ה
  • תְּשַֽׁע־עֶשְׂרֵ֗ה
  • שְׁבַֽע־עֶשְׂרֵ֗ה

355

(ii) Gaʿya in Forms of היה and חיה. Gaʿya is used in many forms from these roots to prevent the slurring over of the he or ḥet. It occurs in two classes of forms:

  1. Before he or ḥet with shewa, as
    • תִֽהְיֶינָה
    • תִֽהְיֶה
    • יִֽהְיֶה
    • יִֽהְיוּ
    • נִֽהְיָתָה
    • וֶֽהְיֵה
    • וִֽהְיוּ
    • מִֽהְיוֹתְךָ
    • בִֽהְיוֹתוֹ
    • לִֽהְיוֹת
    • תִֽהְיוּ
    • and so on (and in the corresponding forms from חיה)
  2. Before the yod of the pronomial prefix where it has shewa, as in
    • וַֽיְחִי
    • וָֽיְהִי

This gaʿya is most commonly used where the word has a disjunctive accent, but may also occur with conjunctives. The tendency to mark it differs in different forms, and it is not consistent in individual MSS, nor is its use uniform in any group of MSS, and it does not appear in the lists of ḥillufim. This gaʿya is, however, as a rule marked consistently when the word in question is joined by maqqef to a word stressed on the first syllable as

Ez 45:8יִֽהְיֶה־לּ֥וֹ
Jer 7:23תִּֽהְיוּ־לִ֣י
Gen 1:5וַֽיְהִי־עֶ֥רֶב וַֽיְהִי־בֹ֖קֶר
