236וְלָראוּבֵנִ֨י 1ve-la-ru-vé-níD3:16

236-1. Jacobson points this word וְלָרֻאוּבֵנִ֨י, i.e. with a qubuts under the resh. In my opinion this is excessive adherence to ל because ל is anomalous here. No reader-friendly edition will be pointed that way. Editions without this qubuts, i.e. editions that are reader-friendly with respect to this particular issue, include Koren, Simanim, Keter Yerushalayim, the 1999 JPS HET, and Dotan’s BHL (main text). (As one would expect, the form with qubuts appears in Appendix A of Dotan’s BHL.)

276עֵדְוֺתָ֤יו 1‘éd-vó-tav
‘é-de-vó-tav		2K23:3

276-1. Jacobson points this word עֵדְוֹתָ֤יו, i.e. with its first vav having a ḥolam dot above it that should be interpreted as ambiguous with regard to the ḥaser/malei distinction. This pointing is expected since in Jacobson the ḥolam dot above vav is always intentionally ambiguous. His treatment of the ḥolam dot above vav is thus analogous to his treatment of the qamats mark. In both cases, he assumes a non-reader-friendly edition. Or at least he wants to prepare the reader for encounters with non-reader-friendly editions. In the table above, in this and all other cases, rather than showing Jacobson’s pointing, we show MAM’s pointing, which distinguishes ḥolam dot “quality” (ḥaser/malei) on vav.

277וְקֹוֵ֥י 1ve-kó-véPs37:9

277-1. Jacobson points this word וְקוֵֹ֥י, presumably to make the point that the ḥaser/malei ambiguity of the ḥolam dot can reach back all the way to the letter before the vav, which in this case is a qof. In other words, Jacobson assumes that there may be no typographic distinctions between the following three cases: (1) ḥolam ḥaser dot on a letter before a vav, (2) ḥolam malei dot on a vav, and (3) ḥolam ḥaser dot on a vav. Or, to put it more mildly, he assumes that the reader may not be able to observe any such typographic distinctions. In other words, he wants to prepare the reader for encounters with (non-reader-friendly) editions that either fail to make these distinctions altogether, or make these distinctions in a way that is so subtle as to be hard to observe.
