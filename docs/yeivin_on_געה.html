‘Gaʿya’ on a Closed Syllable

319

Regular Structure. A word of the same structure as מִתְקַטְּלִים, which has a closed syllable, separated from the stress syllable by another (the “buffer” syllable) followed by vocal shewa (simple or ḥaṭef ) is said to have “regular” structure. In words of such structure, the initial closed syllable has gaʿya if the word has a disjunctive accent, as נִֽתְחַכְּמָ֖ה (Ex 1:10). In the pattern מִתְקַטְּלִים, the vocal shewa stands under a letter with dagesh “forte” (follows a doubled consonant). It may also be the second of two shewa s, in a form of pattern מִתְפַּלְפְּלִים, as הַֽכַּרְמְלִֽי (1S 30:5), or a ḥaṭef shewa in a form of pattern מִתְפַּעֲלִים as מִֽשְׁתַּחֲוִ֖ים (Gen 37:9). Further examples are:-

“Fully Regular” Structure

320

Words of the patterns described in #319 are said to have “fully regular” structure. If a word of this structure has a disjunctive accent, gaʿya is generally marked, but if the accent is conjunctive, gaʿya is generally not marked. This general rule holds good in about 90% of the cases but there are exceptions. In a few dozen cases the accent is disjunctive, but gaʿya is not marked, as

Jud 16:3וַיֶּאֱחֹ֞ז
Neh 1:3מִן־הַשְּׁבִי֙

Dt 14:8...
Gen 34:3וַיֶּאֱהַב֙

“Not Fully Regular” Structure

322

Words with patterns similar to those described in #319 but showing slight differences in structure, are said to be of “not fully regular” structure. Words in this category show slightly less tendency to use gaʿya than words with fully regular structure. The following patterns are included in this category:-

1) Where the closed syllable is followed by a vocal shewa as

1C 2:3הַֽכְּנַעֲנִ֑ית
Ex 16:23אֲשֶֽׁר־תְּבַשְּׁלוּ֙

2) Where the “buffer” syllable has a long vowel, such as qameṣ, followed by a ḥaṭef shewa as

qameṣJos 10:36וַיִּֽלָּחֲמ֖וּ
qameṣ2K 14:10וּֽנְשָׂאֲךָ֖
qameṣ2S 22:8וַיִּֽתְגָּעֲשׁ֖וּ
qameṣ1S 17:36אֶֽת־הָאֲרִ֛י
ṣereGen 32:27אֲשַֽׁלֵּחֲךָ֔
ḥolem1K 8:42וּֽזְרֹעֲךָ֖
ḥolemRut 2:20מִֽגֹּאֲלֵ֖נוּ
ḥolemLev 23:44אֶֽת־מֹעֲדֵ֖י

Words with patterns like xxx (as xxx, xxx, etc.) usually do not have gaʿya and in general the number of exceptions to the rule in this category is much greater than among words with fully regular structure.

3) Where the “buffer” syllable has a long vowel, followed by simple shewa (as in מִתְבָּרְכִים) gaʿya is generally not marked. There are, however, a few exceptions, as

Dt 30:14(and elsewhere)בִּֽלְבָבְךָ֖
Lev 19:5(and elsewhere)לִֽרְצֹנְכֶ֖ם
2S 5:1וּֽבְשָׂרְךָ֖

4) Where the stress syllable is preceded by a ḥaṭef--or even a simple shewa--which separates two identical consonants (or a $xatef under some other non-guttural letter). If this $xatef is preceded by a short vowel, gaʿya is marked as in words with fully regular structure, as

Jud 16:24אבג
1K 8:30אבג

If the vowel preceding the ḥaṭef is long, the marking of gaʿya follows the rule given in (2) above, as

Zeph 2:1אבג
Jos 22:6אבג
Dt 32:6אבג
