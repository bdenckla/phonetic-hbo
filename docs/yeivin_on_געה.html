‘Gaʿya’ on a Closed Syllable

319

Regular Structure. A word of the same structure as מִתְקַטְּלִים, which has a closed syllable, separated from the stress syllable by another (the “buffer” syllable) followed by vocal shewa (simple or ḥatef ) is said to have “regular” structure. In words of such structure, the initial closed syllable has gaʿya if the word has a disjunctive accent, as נִֽתְחַכְּמָ֖ה (Ex 1:10). In the pattern מִתְקַטְּלִים, the vocal shewa stands under a letter with dagesh “forte” (follows a doubled consonant). It may also be the second of two shewa s, in a form of pattern מִתְפַּלְפְּלִים, as הַֽכַּרְמְלִֽי (1S 30:5), or a ḥatef shewa in a form of pattern מִתְפַּעֲלִים as מִֽשְׁתַּחֲוִ֖ים (Gen 37:9). “Fully Regular” Structure

320

Words of the patterns described in #319 are said to have “fully regular” structure. If a word of this structure has a disjunctive accent, gaʿya is generally marked, but if the accent is conjunctive, gaʿya is generally not marked. This general rule holds good in about 90% of the cases but there are exceptions. In a few dozen cases the accent is disjunctive, but gaʿya is not marked, as
