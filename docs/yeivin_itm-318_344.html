Musical Gaʿya

318

The names “heavy gaʿya” and “light gaʿya” (געיה כבדה and געיה קלה) for the two basic categories of musical gaʿya were introduced by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan, and were, as Dotan has remarked, an unfortunate innovation. Dotan, in his edition of the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim uses the terms “small gaʿya” and “great gaʿya” (געיה קטנה and געיה גדולה), terms which are used in the Masorah. These terms are not appropriate either, for although in the Masorah the term “small gaʿya” is used for gaʿya on a closed syllable, it may also include gaʿya on an open syllable, and it seems quite likely that the term “great gaʿya” in the Masorah refers to an accent. For this reason the descriptive names “gaʿya on a closed syllable” and “gaʿya on an open syllable” are used here.

“Fully Regular” Structure

320

Words of the three patterns described in #319 are said to have fully regular structure. As mentioned above, if a word of this structure has a disjunctive accent, gaʿya is generally marked, but if the accent is conjunctive, gaʿya is generally not marked. This general rule holds good in about 90% of the cases but there are exceptions. In a few dozen cases the accent is disjunctive, but gaʿya is not marked, as

“Almost Fully Regular” Structure

322

Words with patterns similar to those described in #319, but showing slight differences in structure, are still considered to have regular structure, though their structure is not fully regular. We refer to such words as having “almost fully regular” (AFR) structure. Words in this category show slightly less tendency to use gaʿya than words with fully regular structure. The following four patterns are included in this category:-

AFR1: Words of this pattern would conform to one of the three fully regular patterns were it not for the fact that their buffer syllable has vocal shewa attached to it.

1C 2:3הַֽכְּנַעֲנִ֑יתהַֽככְּנַעֲנִ֑ית≈FR3
Ex 16:23אֲשֶֽׁר־תְּבַשְּׁלוּ֙אֲשֶֽׁר־תְּבַששְּׁלוּ֙≈FR1

(We now see that in words of fully regular structure, the buffer syllable must not have a vocal shewa attached to it.)

AFR2: Words of this pattern would conform to FR3 were it not for the fact that their buffer syllable has a long vowel, such as qameṣ. (Recall that the buffer syllable of FR3 is constrained to have a short vowel.)

qameṣJos 10:36וַיִּֽלָּחֲמ֖וּוַייִּֽללָּחֲמ֖וּ

qameṣ2S 22:8וַיִּֽתְגָּעֲשׁ֖וּוַייִּֽתְגָּעֲשׁ֖וּ
qameṣ1S 17:36אֶֽת־הָאֲרִ֛יאֶֽת־הָאֲרִ֛י
ṣereGen 32:27אֲשַֽׁלֵּחֲךָ֔אֲשַֽׁללֵּחֲךָ֔
ḥolem1K 8:42וּֽזְרֹעֲךָ֖וּֽזְרֹעֲךָ֖
ḥolemRut 2:20מִֽגֹּאֲלֵ֖נוּמִֽגגֹּאֲלֵ֖נוּ
ḥolemLev 23:44אֶֽת־מֹעֲדֵ֖יאֶֽת־מֹעֲדֵ֖י

Words of pattern AFR2 like הַפּוֹעֲלִים (as הַכֹּהֲנִים, הַשֹּׁעֲרִים, etc.) usually do not have gaʿya and in general the number of exceptions to the rule in this category is much greater than among words with fully regular structure.

AFR3: Words of this pattern have:

  • A buffer syllable that is open and long-vowelled.
  • A vocal shewa that is notated as a simple shewa.

An example of this pattern is מִתְבָּרְכִים. In words of this pattern, gaʿya is generally not marked. There are, however, a few exceptions, as

Jos 22:6וַֽיְבָרְכֵ֖םוַֽיְבָרְכֵ֖ם
Dt 30:14 AEבִּֽלְבָבְךָ֖בִּֽלְבָבְךָ֖
Lev 19:5 AEלִֽרְצֹנְכֶ֖םלִֽרְצֹנְכֶ֖ם
2S 5:1וּֽבְשָׂרְךָ֖וּֽבְשָׂרְךָ֖

AFR4: Words of this pattern have:

  • A buffer syllable that is open, with either a short or long vowel.
  • A vocal shewa whose letter is the first of an identical pair. (The stress syllable starts with the second letter of this pair.)

Examples of pattern AFR4 include the following:

Jud 16:24וַֽיְהַלְל֖וּוַֽיְהַלְל֖וּ
1K 8:30יִֽתְפַּלְל֖וּיִֽתְפַּלְל֖וּ
Zeph 2:1הִֽתְקוֹשְׁשׁ֖וּהִֽתְקוֹשְׁשׁ֖וּ
Dt 32:6וַֽיְכֹנְנֶֽךָ׃וַֽיְכֹנְנֶֽךָ׃
Is 24:19הִֽתְרֹעֲעָ֖ההִֽתְרֹעֲעָ֖ה

323

Gaʿya is sometimes marked on a closed syllable in words with the following non-regular structure:

  • A buffer syllable that has, or could have, gaʿya.
  • No vocal shewa, i.e. the stress syllable is preceded directly by the buffer syllable.

(While this structure is non-regular, it is close enough to regular that we mention it here rather than in the next section, which covers cases where gaʿya is marked on a closed syllable in more arbitrarily non-regular words.)

Words of this “buffer could have gaʿya, no vocal shewa” category include the following two sub-categories:

1) Words of a closed syllable followed by some form from the root היה or חיה which could have gaʿya on the first syllable (#355), as Is 10:22 אִם־יִהְיֶ֞ה — bN has אִֽם־.

2) Words having a buffer syllable marked with gaʿya and closed by a guttural (#354). E.g.:

1C 5:32וּֽזְרַֽחְיָ֖הוּֽזְרַֽחְיָ֖ה
1C 27:19יִֽשְׁמַֽעְיָ֖הוּיִֽשְׁמַֽעְיָ֖הוּ
1S 26:19יִֽשְׁמַֽע־נָא֙יִֽשְׁמַֽע־נָא֙
Jer 5:7אֶֽסְלַֽח־לָ֔ךְאֶֽסְלַֽח־לָ֔ךְ

(The ketiv of אֶֽסְלַֽח־ (Jer 5:7) is אסלוח. We note this merely to be thorough; it is not relevant to the point at hand.)

(In all four examples above, the first gaʿya is musical, the second phonetic.)

(Note that we consider the initial shureq in וּֽזְרַֽחְיָ֖ה (1C 5:32) to start a closed syllable with a short vowel. See #349.)

These “buffer could have gaʿya, no vocal shewa” words are somewhat similar to words of pattern מִתְבָּרְכִים (AFR3 in #322), since there, too, the buffer syllable ( בָּ ) could have gaʿya (albeit on an open syllable).

Non-regular Structure

324

The most common use of gaʿya on a closed syllable is in words of regular structure. Words with structure which is non-regular — i.e. words that are not patterned like מִתְקַטְּלִים or its variants described above — rarely show gaʿya on a closed syllable. There are no fixed rules for the use of gaʿya on such words.

1) In some 15 cases there is a gaʿya on the fourth syllable before the stress. E.g.:

Dt 12:2אֶֽת־כׇּל־הַמְּקֹמ֞וֹתאֶֽת־כׇּל־הַממְּקֹמ֞וֹת

Jud 18:2מִֽמִּשְׁפַּחְתָּ֡םמִֽממִּשְׁפַּחְתָּ֡ם

Gaʿya before Paseq

325

This is most commonly found in cases like 1K 2:30 וַיֹּ֥אמֶֽר ׀ לֹ֖א, where it is evidently used to mark a separation between the two words greater than that indicated by the paseq. Gaʿya is only marked in this position in early MSS, and is rare even there.

Gaʿya-OSR

326

Gaʿya may be marked on syllables of the following types:

  • Open, with either a long or a short vowel.
  • Shewa-closed, with a long vowel.

This is the gaʿya called “light” by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan, and “great” by Dotan. We refer to it as gaʿya-OSR, where OSR stands for

  • [on an] Open Syllable
  • [or on the] Related [case of a shewa-closed, long-vowelled syllable]

With some exceptions (#352, #386), the Masoretes regarded shewa after a long vowel within a word as silent (לא יצא בפה). So, in a word like קָֽטְלוּ , the syllable marked by gaʿya is, in their view, closed, not open. However, because the rules for gaʿya in this situation are similar to those for its use on an open syllable, the two situations are described together. Note, however, that two different types of syllable are involved.

We divide the main uses of gaʿya-OSR into the following three cases:

The use of gaʿya on an open syllable is not described in the masoretic literature or in other early sources, and there are only scattered references to this gaʿya. In the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim (Baer-Strack 1879, #32) it is said

י(י)ראוmeaning “fear” has gaʿya asGen 20:8וַיִּֽירְא֥וּ
יראוmeaning “see” has no gaʿya asNu 17:24וַיִּרְא֥וּ

In the Horayat ha-Qore p. 90 (Dérenbourg, 1870, p. 398) it is said that

Gaʿya sometimes distinguishes meaning, as in the rule “forms from ירא have gaʿya, forms from ראה do not” and as in תִּשְׁנ֕וּ (Neh 13:21) which means “do again” (שנה) and יִֽ֭שְׁנוּ (Prov 4:16) which means “sleep” (ישן). Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan was the first to draw up rules for the use of this gaʿya, and he has been followed by later scholars.

331

Gaʿya-OSR is the most common category of gaʿya in the Bible, and occurs in thousands of words. In the early MSS, this gaʿya is not marked on all the words where it could be, but only on some of them, and in some MSS it is marked more commonly than in others. It seems probable that it was not considered important to mark it — possibly because it made less difference to the pronunciation of the word than did gaʿya on a closed syllable.

In MsA, this gaʿya is most commonly marked on words with pashṭa or zaqef, less commonly on those with other disjunctives, and only rarely on words with conjunctives. It is marked in about 30% of the possible cases.

In MsL and MsS it is marked in about 40% of the possible cases. In MsB and MsS1 it is marked in about 20% of the possible cases. In MsC this gaʿya is marked much more commonly — in about 75% of the possible cases.

The other early MSS mark this gaʿya in varying proportions of the possible cases. It is not always true that earlier MSS mark it less, as is shown by MsB and MsC, which are roughly contemporary. It is, however, generally true that later MSS mark this gaʿya more commonly, and the printed texts mark gaʿya on an open syllable regularly on every syllable suitable for it.

Gaʿya on an Open, Post-accent Syllable

Gaʿya with Shewa

333

Gaʿya marked with either simple shewa or ḥaṭef shewa at the beginning of a word indicates (as gaʿya always does) that the syllable must be slowed or lengthened. Consequently that shewa becomes a vowel — probably equivalent to an ordinary short vowel. This gaʿya is rare in the twenty one books (only some 200 cases occur) but it is common in the three books. This is a musical gaʿya, but it is often used before a guttural, which suggests that there may be phonetic reasons for its use. The system of marking it is similar to that for gaʿya on a closed syllable, but there are no firm rules for its use.

This gaʿya is marked on the second, third, or fourth syllable before the stress. It is usually used with “high” accents (#195), as we see in the examples below, with gershayim, pazer, telisha, and zarqa.

Gaʿya with shewa is regularly marked in early MSS but the rules for it are not described in early sources, nor even by Yequtiʾel ha-Naqdan. Heidenheim and Baer were the first to establish these rules. The pronunciation of shewa with gaʿya is described in the masoretic literature (as in the Diqduqe ha-Ṭeʿamim (Baer-Strack 1879, #11) — see also Morag, 1963, p. 160 ff.). They state that shewa at the beginning of a word was generally pronounced as a small pataḥ (פתחה קטנה) but that when it was marked with gaʿya it was pronounced like a full pataḥ (בפתחה גדולה תצא). Before gutturals, the shewa was pronounced as a short vowel of the same quality as the vowel following the guttural, but when it was marked with gaʿya it had the sound of a full vowel of that quality (thus בְּֽהֹנוֹת֩ (Jud 1:7) would be pronounced with three ḥolem s). Shewa at the beginning of a word before yod was pronounced like a short ḥireq, but when marked with a gaʿya, it was pronounced like a full ḥireq.

Gaʿya on a Maqqef-closed, Long-vowelled Syllable

337

Gaʿya on a shewa-closed, long-vowelled syllable is covered in #326. Gaʿya may also be marked on a maqqef-closed, long-vowelled syllable. This can occur before either a disjunctive or a conjunctive accent.

This gaʿya is marked regularly in most printed texts, but in MSS it is usually marked only if the candidate syllable stands right before the stress syllable, as

Hos 8:7אֵֽין־ל֗וֹ

If the candidate syllable is the second before the stress, gaʿya is usually not marked, as

If the candidate syllable is the third before the stress, gaʿya is more likely to be marked, as

In general the marking of this gaʿya in the early MSS is more regular than the marking of gaʿya on an open syllable, but less regular than the marking of gaʿya on a closed syllable of the regular type. However, the regularity varies from MS to MS.

Gaʿya on a Closed, Ṣere-vowelled, Post-accent Syllable

Where a word ending in a closed syllable pointed with ṣere has its stress retracted, and the ṣere remains, it is marked by gaʿya (#308, 1c), as

Is 66:3עֹ֣רֵֽף כֶּ֔לֶב

Is 40:7נָ֣בֵֽל צִ֔יץcf. 40:8

This gaʿya is marked both in MSS and in printed texts.

The System of Preference for Musical Gaʿya

339

In early MSS, such as MsA, MsL, MsC, two gaʿya s are only rarely marked on the same word (or on a group of words joined by maqqef ). Where a word or word group could have more than one gaʿya, only one of them is generally marked. For example, in the following two words, gaʿya is marked under the initial shewa but not on the following open syllable:

Ezra 9:12בְּֽנוֹתֵיכֶ֞ם
Jer 31:20 ISEאֲֽרַחֲמֶ֖נּוּ

(Above, we mark Jer 31:20 with “ISE” because it is only Jer 31:20 in some editions; others have it as 31:19.)

In Ex 15:26 כׇּֽל־הַמַּחֲלָ֞ה , gaʿya is marked on the initial closed syllable, despite the fact that, given the word’s regular structure, we would expect gaʿya to marked on the closed syllable starting with he.

Conversely, in MsA in 2K 23:12 וְאֶת־הַֽמִּזְבְּח֡וֹת , gaʿya is marked on the closed syllable we would expect, given the word’s regular structure. Gaʿya is not marked on the following two other candidate locations:

  • On the initial closed syllable, under the alef.
  • On the initial shewa.

(Gaʿya is marked on the initial shewa by bN.)

340

The choice of which of two or more gaʿya s should be marked under a word is determined by certain general principles, although these are affected by many detailed considerations which cannot be described here. The basic principle is that gaʿya on a closed syllable is given preference over gaʿya-OSR. Examples are:-

Jud 18:12וַֽיַּעֲל֗וּ
Jud 19:29הַֽמַּאֲכֶ֙לֶת֙

Here gaʿya on the initial closed syllable (of the regular type) is marked, but that on the following open syllable (before the ḥaṭef ) is not. So also

Dt 31:20אֶֽל־הָאֲדָמָ֣ה׀
Nu 17:21כׇּֽל־נְשִׂיאֵיהֶ֡ם

Here gaʿya is marked on the initial closed syllable of non-regular type, but not on the following open syllable. This general principle is common to all the early MSS.

On the problem of preference in words which could have more than one gaʿya-OSR, see #328.

341

In the case of words which could have gaʿya on an open syllable and on a maqqef-closed, long-vowelled syllable (#337) the general rule is that if the closed syllable comes right before the stress syllable, gaʿya is marked on it, as

Rut 1:11הַעֽוֹד־לִ֤י
Job 2:11לָנֽוּד־ל֖וֹ

However, if this is not the case, the gaʿya on the open syllable is preferred, as

Is 8:11 AEהָֽעָם־הַזֶּ֖ה
Jos 11:12הַמְּלָֽכִים־הָ֠אֵ֠לֶּה
Dt 7:1גּֽוֹיִם־רַבִּ֣ים׀ אא֡א

The Marking of Gaʿya in Manuscripts and Printed Texts

342

In early MSS, gaʿya on a closed syllable of regular or non-regular type, and gaʿya with shewa, are usually carefully and accurately marked. In some MSS this is also the case with gaʿya on a maqqef-closed, long-vowelled syllable. MSS following this system show the following characteristics:

  1. The system of marking these gaʿya s is generally consistent throughout the MS.
  2. The system of marking these gaʿya s is similar in different MSS. That is, gaʿya is generally marked in the same position on the same word in nearly all MSS.
  3. Variations involving gaʿya noted in lists of ḥillufim or in marginal notes in the MSS are almost solely concerned with gaʿya in these categories.

These characteristics show the importance to the Masoretes of gaʿya in these categories, as opposed to gaʿya-OSR. The lesser importance of gaʿya-OSR is also shown by the fact that

  1. The system of marking gaʿya-OSR is not consistent in any single MS.
  2. The early MSS do not share the same system for marking gaʿya in these categories, and they show no uniformity in the words on which gaʿya in these categories is marked.
  3. Lists of ḥillufim are almost bare of examples of variants involving gaʿya in these categories.

343

In later MSS and in printed texts, a different system of marking gaʿya is used. In printed texts, gaʿya-OSR is generally consistently and systematically marked, but gaʿya on a closed syllable is marked only sporadically, or not at all.

344

This difference is reflected in the system of preference for marking gaʿya. If a word could have gaʿya both on a closed syllable and on an open syllable, that on the open syllable is marked in printed text, as Ex 14:19 וַיַּֽעֲמֹ֖ד whereas that on the closed syllable would be marked in early MSS, i.e. וַֽיַּעֲמֹ֖ד.

In a number of scholarly editions, such as those of Baer, Heidenheim, Qoren (Koren), and others, gaʿya is marked as completely as possible, so that both gaʿya s are marked in a word like וַֽיַּֽעֲמֹ֖ד. Similar usage is found in a number of MSS, as noted by Eliahu ha-Levi in his Ṭuv Ṭaʿam chapter 7:

When there is dagesh in the second letter of a word, and this letter is followed by א, ה, ח, or ע pointed with ḥaṭef pataḥ, then that word has two metheg s: one on the first letter  —  the principal metheg, one on the letter before the guttural — but only if the word has a disjunctive accent, as וַֽיַּֽעֲמֹד and וַֽיַּֽעֲבֹד.
